Allama Sayyid Imdad Hussain Kazmi al-Mashhadi (1901–1975) was an intellectual, scholar, writer and speaker. Born in Gujrat (Punjab), he was the son of Sayyid Abbas Ali Shah Kazmi al‑Mashhadi. Through his father’s family — connected to the Awadh–Lucknow scholarly tradition — he inherited a lineage shaped by the great centres of Shia learning in North India. He is buried in the Kadhimiya Cemetery near his family home in the Noorpur Sayyidan area of the city.

His grandfather, Mir Sayyid Ramzan Ali Kazmi al-Mashhadi, was active in religious circles and was known in Lucknow and Amroha for his majalis khwani (recitation at religious gatherings). He had scholarly contact with Allama Sayyid Hashmat Ali Khairullahpuri(1858–1943), an Indian Shia jurist who spent a formative period in Lahore studying Arabic and Persian and later studied and taught in Lucknow, Iraq, Iran, and Turkey. Mir Sayyid Ramzan Ali Kazmi spent much of his life in the religious environments of North India and was referred to by the title Sultan ul Zakireen (“King of Reciters”).

His paternal uncle, Sayyid Farzand Ali Kazmi, was among the students of Allama Hakeem Sayyid Ghulam-ul-Hasnain Kanturi (c.1832–1919), a Lucknow based scholar whose work covered classical Islamic sciences, philosophy, and medicine. Farzand Ali Kazmi studied at Kanturi’s Madressa Imania. Kantur represents one of the intellectual pipelines that supplied Lucknow’s Shia scholarly culture, and its scholars influenced regions far beyond Awadh, including Punjab and the Indo‑Pakistani Shia networks

Allama Imdad received his initial education in Wazirabad, then Gujranwala, and later Lahore. He passed the Munshi Fazil, Maulvi Fazil, and Adeeb Fazil examinations from Punjab University and subsequently obtained a B.A. degree. Scholars at Jamia Nazmia, Lucknow (founded in 1890 by Ayatullah Sayyid Najmul Hasan), awarded him an honorary certificate titled “Shams ul Afazil.” He also passed the Gurmukhi examination and obtained a Giani certificate.

Although employed in government service, he pursued religious scholarship and endeavours. He delivered sermons in Jammu & Kashmir, and in Poonch, including during the rule of Raja Jagat Dev Singh (1928–1940). The Poonch administration conferred on him the title “Abul Fazl Sani” (“The Second Abul Fazl”) and presented him with a robe of honour and a certificate. During his speaking visits to Poonch, he stayed as a guest of the royal household. He also spoke in Assam, Madras, Bengal, the Central Provinces, and the United Provinces. He received numerous ijazaat (authorisations/licenses) from scholars in Iraq, Pakistan, and India.

His literary work appeared in various Indian newspapers, and he served as editor of the Ahl-e-Sunnat magazine Sufi. In addition to Punjabi and Urdu, he was proficient in English, Arabic, and Persian. His English poems and articles were published in the London based magazine Victory and the Delhi based Postal Comrade. In the late 1950s, his academic and research writings were published in the monthly journal Ma'arif e Islam.

Major Works and Writings

Allama Imdad’s Urdu translation of the Qur’an with annotations, Tafsir ul Muttaqeen, was published by Insaf Publishers, Lahore. His other works include Tafsir b'il Ra’y, Tahqeeq-e-Mahdi, Al Fatima, Barakat-e-Muharram bajawab Bid‘at-e-Muharram, Tatbiq-ush-Shahadat, A‘mal-e-Wajibiya, Mu‘allim-ul-Islam, and Akhlaq-ul-Ma‘sumeen. A large manuscript, Istiqlal-e-Haq-e-Azadari (approximately 1500 pages), discusses various aspects of the rituals and practices associated with the mourning of Imam Husayn.

Idara Ma'arif-e-Islam (Lahore), Diaspora & International Chapters

Idara Ma'arif-e-Islam was established in Lahore in the mid twentieth century as a Shia scholarly institution and publisher. Allama Imdad Kazmi became an active member of the organisation and served as Chief Supervisor of its monthly journal from the late 1950s until the mid 1970s, including four years as its President. During this period, the Idara’s publications and scholarly activities contributed to the formation of new international chapters in England and East Pakistan.

A report published in Ma'arif-e-Islam (Volume 7, Issue 4, p.8, July 1961), records correspondence from the England Chapter to the Central Office in Lahore. According to this report, among the earliest public Muharram majalis and mourning commemorations in Britain were organised in Birmingham in 1961, drawing more than forty participants and visitors from other cities. The majalis were addressed by Nazir Hussain and Fida Hussain, who were touring England with the troupe of the Punjabi folk artist Muhammad Alam Lohar. Alam Lohar’s performances, including Dāstān e Karbala, played a role in introducing the narrative of Karbala into Punjabi folk culture and making it accessible to wider audiences in Pakistan and globally.

The same issue of the journal (Volume 7, Issue 4, p.10, July 1961) includes a report from the East Pakistan Chapter, Tejgaon, Dhaka. It notes that during Muharram, the Idara’s Bengali language booklet Aqaid-e-Shia (Shia Beliefs) was distributed free of charge in majalis, mosques, madrasas, and local neighbourhoods. The booklet outlined key aspects of Ja'fari belief and addressed common public misunderstandings about Shi‘ism. It also provided concise information on the birth and martyrdom dates of the Fourteen Infallibles and referenced scholars of Ahl al Sunnah wal Jama‘ah and contemporaries of their respective eras.
